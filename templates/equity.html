Increasing Access to Zero Emission Vehicles

The adoption of zero emission vehicles is more complicated than just people purchasing them. An infrastructure needs to be in place to accomodate for this increased use of ZEVs -- the most obvious being the electrical grid.

Here we explore more about the electical grid, its stress under extreme weather, and its ability to take on increased load of EV charging.

Stressors to the grid make it difficult to support ZEV adoption

Grid Overview


This is the basic framework of the electrical grid. It includes generators, lines, and substations. When the grid is stressed, a breakdown of any of these parts will affect the ability to provide electricity to industry, commerical, and residental.

What stresses the grid?


Extreme Weather as a Grid Stressor


Extreme weather negatively impacts ability to provide consistent electricity.

Burillo et al. (2019) conducted a study focusing on current and future stresses to electricity infrastructure in Los Angeles County, California.
The case study describes the effect that rising air temperatures have on the vulnerability of LA County’s electricity infrastructure. See figure right.

How does this relate to ZEV access?
In extreme weather events, electricity might need to be shut off preventing people to charge their vehicles during the duration of the outage. This might be ok for outages lasting less than day, but outages could last for days on end.

Example: January 2023 California Power Outages


Most recently we've seen California's grid stressed under extreme weather conditions of winter storms, high winds, and heavy rainfall. Thousands of Californian's were without power in their homes as powerlines and support poles were taken down with fallen trees.

According to LA Times, over 400,000 people were without power at one time. And power did not return for days for some communities, with power outages lasting over 4 consecutive days.

At-Home ZEV Charging as a Grid Stressor


A large portion of electric grid infrastructure in California is ill-equipped to handle at-home electric vehicle charging.

More than half of households in California lack access to new loads of electricity for EV Charging:

In order to meet the demands of additional energy-intensive applications, such as electric vehicle charging, the electrical grid would need to make upgrades or households would need to turn to increased solar photovoltaic adoption in order to make up for the additional load.

Even then, the grid also may not have adequate hosting capacity for many households to install or adopt solar PV (Brockway, Conde, and Callaway, 2021).

Grid constraints on solar PV adoption include thermal constraints, power quality constraints, protection grid constraints, as well as physical building suitability for PV installation.

Access to public charging affects adoption of ZEVs.


Availability of charging infrastructure can play a large role in if an individual considers purchasing an electric vehicle because having access to charging is essential in the operation of the vehicle.

People who are in the most need of access to public chargers include:

  • People without off-street parking
  • People without home chargers
  • Apartment residents who have lower access to home chargers
  • People who rent homes without chargers– renters may not want to pay for the upgrade themselves and landlords may not want to pay for an upgrade they will not use
  • People who are traveling long distances and have range anxiety.

Often the people who match the descriptions of those who are in the most need of access to public chargers are located in poorer or more disadvantaged communities. Hsu and Fingerman (2021) found that public electric vehicle charger access generally increases with income. In addition, gaps in public electric vehicle access also exist among different racial groups.

What is being done to increase access to charging?

1

As part of the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act, which was signed into law on November 15, 2021, $7.5 billion was dedicated to building a national network of 500 thousand electric vehicle chargers, with $383 million allocated for California.

2

California’s 2022-23 budget dedicated $600 million over four years for ZEV Fueling Infrastructure Grants that prioritize fast chargers (California Legislative Analyst’s Office, 2022).
